Before looking at these techniques, one of the best things to do first is documenting what you have to on either digitally or paper!



pomodoro

The Pomodoro Technique is created by Francesco Cirillo for a more productive way to work and study. The technique uses a timer to break down work into intervals, traditionally 25 minutes in length, separated by short breaks. Each interval is known as a pomodoro, from the Italian word for 'tomato', after the tomato-shaped kitchen timer that Cirillo used as a university student. - Wikipedia

kanban

Kanban is when you split your to do list into three categories, use this method digitally:

  • To do: all tasks that need to get done soon
  • In progress: tasks you're currently working on
  • Done: tasks already finished

time blocking

Time blocking is a time management method that divides work sessions into sections and assigns particular tasks to each section.

eisenhower matrix

This easy prioritization system labels task priorities based on their urgency and importance. The method uses four quadrants to classify tasks:

  • Important and Urgent
  • Not important and urgent
  • Important but not urgent
  • Not important or urgent

Pareto Principles (80/20 rule)

It implies that 80% of our total productivity comes from only 20% of our efforts, so that 80% of your money may come from 20% of your work. It's about determining which of our duties offers the highest return and prioritizing them; focusing our efforts on the "right work" rather to wasting time on small things. The 80/20 rule is intended to bring purpose and worth to your day by pushing you to prioritize what is important.

Rule of Three

The technique simply requires jotting down the top three things you want to do each day, which is especially useful if you have a lengthy, looming to-do list. By starting your day with three significant aims, you're constantly reminding yourself of what's essential and where your attention should be directed.

two-minute rule

David Allen's book, Getting Things Done, suggests the two-minute rule, which encourages immediate action on tasks that take less than two minutes, to combat procrastination and improve productivity.
